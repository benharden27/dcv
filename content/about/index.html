This Data Communication and Visualization class is built to help prepare you for the data research and communication work you will be doing in your future careers by building skills in:

  • Data Visualization in the R programming language
  • Understanding good data and visual design strategies
  • Optimizing your verbal communication of data

The class will begin on October 4th, 2021 and run until the end of the program on December 23rd, 2021, but the majority of the classwork will be concluded by the end of the shore component (November 15th, 2021).

Class Philosophy

Before I get to details, I want to start by laying out my philosophy for this class.

You are all coming to this class with different experiences. Some of you have had previous research opportunities, some none. Others will have been exposed to coding skills, many will not. Some of you will be oceanography majors, while for others this will be a new topic to grapple with. Regardless of your previous experiences, you all have strengths to bring to the table. You all have a responsibility to keep your eyes open for your peers who are struggling and help them out. This class is a collaborative space so that everyone can grow their skills and understanding as much as possible. No one succeeds because someone else fails. We all grow together.

The class will be mostly self-paced, meaning you are free to move through the content and exercises when it suits you. My reasons for this are to help you:

  • Plan your time to best suit your learning style and energy levels
  • Do work in timezone when others (including me) will not be free/working during initial virtual component of the program.
  • Have more time for things you struggle with by allowing you to go faster through things you might find more straight-forward
  • Have more agency about how you spend your time and take breaks when you need them
  • Not get too focused keeping track of many deadlines
  • Collaborate with your peers at times that suit you

Alongside the self-paces work will be scheduled portions of the class that will include group work, discussions, presentation, etc. More on the structure below.

I value feedback and self-reflection. You will be given many opportunities to let me know how you are getting on, what you are understanding, and what you have questions about. My hope is that through this process that you become conscious of your strengths and areas for growth. You will also be helping me understand how best to help you and your fellow students. For the duration of the class, you are my primary professional activity so feel empowered to contact me if you have any questions or concerns.

What I expect from you

I hope you will see this class as more than simply a requirement to gain a grade for college credit. It should be a useful tool for you to advance your thinking and skills around communicating data effectively.

I expect you to use your time in the class effectively. The self-paced mode will give you lots of flexibility to work when and how you like, but I do expect you to engage with all aspects. We will meet most days of the week for a total of 7 hours of weekly contact time. You should expect to spend a comparable amount of time on your work out of class. Over the 7-day week, you should be spending an average of 2 hours a day on your DCV work. Use your peers as a support network for things you are finding hard and to keep you accountable.

I welcome your feedback (and will ask for it frequently) so please let me know how you are getting on and what isn’t sticking and I will try and help you out. Please also let us know how long the classwork is taking you so I can make sure I am calibrating the quantiy and depth of content accordingly.

What you can expect from me

I will be available for immediate feedback and discussion during the near-daily class period (see below) and during out of class office hours. Please speak up if you need specific support during any of these times - the more heads-up I can have the more effective I can be at helping you. However, please be patient as I try to meet the needs of the whole class. If there are enough questions or interest in a particular topic then I will arrange small group instruction.

Outside the class window we I also be available to answer questions and give feedback. Please contact me by email which I check daily from 8am to 8pm. I am unavailable from sundown on Friday to sundown on Saturday.

Class Structure

Section, Modules and Levels (and Grades)

The class content on this site is organized into three topics:

  • Visualize: learning how to effectively use R to create compelling graphics
  • Design: Introduction to key elements of data design and data presentations
  • Communicate: Increasing your communication capactity through radio storytelling

Each topic is composed of a set of modules, each with four levels of work. Topics do not need to be completed in order, but modules and levels are typically sequential with an assignment/assessment at the end of each level that needs to be completed before you can move on. The level pages themselves will provide clear instructions for what you have to do.

I am expecting everyone to engage with every module to Level 3. Level 4 is optional. You do not need to complete all Level 4 work - you are free to skip it on any module (but you will probably need to complete some if you want to maintain an A average). You are free to get feedback and revise any assignment up until the end of the class - the gradebook for most module content (other than your final at-sea assignments) will stay open until you board the shop on November 15th.

When you have completed the work from a level, you need to let us know by clicking the “Exit Ticket” button at the bottom of the level page and giving a small amount of reflection/feedback. This will notify us that there is work that needs to be reviewed. Until you get feedback, you are free to move on to another topic/level.

Each topic has a culminating project which is grade-weighted twice that of the other modules in the topic.

Grading-wise, the typical grade structure for most modules is as follows:

Level To Move on… Module Grade
1 Sign-off by instructor 70%
2 Sign-off by instructor 80%
3 Score of 6 or more 80% + level 3 score (out of 10)
4 N/A 80% + level 3 score + level 4 score

Levels 1-2 are pass/not-pass whereas levels 3 and 4 are nominally scored (although not always).

Your final grade will be calculated based on the weighted scores in each module. Each standard module carries the same weight with project modules carrying twice that weight. As a result, your total class grade will be calculated with the following weights:

Staying On-pace

You are free to move at a pace that suits you for the most part. However, there will be a number of fixed-point assessments, discussions and presentations. These will be generally tied to the completion of level 3 work and may be collaborative - you should keep an eye on the dates/times of these so you can make sure you move through your levels in time to fully participate.

To help you there will be a public tracker that will show which levels you and your peers have completed. It will also have a “pace-setter” - my sense of where you should be in any module on any day to be ready for the fixed-point activities. Please use this to help you gauge what you might need to do next and to see which of your peers might be able to help you with work if you are stuck.

Class Interaction

Class will be 12pm to 3pm Eastern Daily. This is a broad period in which we’ll have both synchronous and asynchronous class work. You instructors will be available to you during this time and we expect you to be available for full-class, small group and individual interaction as needed.

Class will begin every day at 12pm with a short survey and check-in session. This space will be used for announcements, making a plan for the rest of class, and gathering topics that might need small-group or individual attention.

After the initial full-group check-in will be free time to work. We will be available at this time for questions and assistance and will also be scheduling in small group check-ins, one-on-ones and other activities at this time to support students with their work.

During free-work periods, you should feel empowered to take short breaks, grab some food, step outside to get some air, etc. However, this is our time to work with you and we expect you to be available to engage with us if we need to get hold of you.

2pm to 3pm will be designated as the time for full-group assessments, discussions and presentations. Please be fully available during this time if there is something scheduled. The calendar of these events will be kept current in the PEP 2021 Google Calendar.

Outside of the 12pm-3pm window, your instructors will also be available to field questions and offer assistance from 9am to 5pm Daily. Please post your questions in the #class Slack Channel and be patient for a reply. Remember, your peers will often be a great resource for you - please work together if you like, but remember to only submit your own work (see policy on academic accuracy in the syllabus).

Resources/Texts

Our general oceanography text will be:

Introduction to Ocean Sciences, Second digital edition, ver 4.14 by Douglas A. Segar

This is a good introductory text on the many aspects of ocean science and is all available online for free. We’ll be directing you to readings by chapter number and page. In addition, there is a section called Critical Concepts which covers ideas that span multiple chapters. We will be directing you to review some of those by number also. The chapters all load as PDFs so you might consider saving assigned chapters to your computer and organizing them for future browsing.

For R visualization work, the core text is:

R for Data Science by Garrett Grolemund and Hadley Wickham. This is a great intro text to working with data in R. I recommend reading the whole thing over the course of the summer, but I’ll be assigning specific chapters in class.

Other readings and resources for the class will be distributed as required.
